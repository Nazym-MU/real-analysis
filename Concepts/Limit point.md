A point $x$ is a limit point of a set $A$ if every $\epsilon$-neighborhood $V_\epsilon(x)$ of $x$ intersects the set $A$ at some point other than $x$.


## Example
1. Consider the set $\mathbb Q ⊆ \mathbb R$ of rational numbers. Its set of limit points is actually all of $\mathbb R$. Let $y ∈ \mathbb R$ be arbitrary, and consider any neighborhood $V_\epsilon (y) = (y− ϵ, y + ϵ)$. By [[Theorem 1.4.3. Density of Q in R]], there exists a rational number $r \neq y$ that falls in this neighborhood. Thus, $y$ is a limit point of $\mathbb Q$.
2. Every $\mathbb R$  is the limit of a sequence of $\mathbb I$.
