A sequence $(a_n)$ is called a Cauchy sequence if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists$ and $N\in \mathbb N$ such that whenever $m, n \geq N$ it follows that $|a_n - a_m| < \epsilon$.
Similar to the definition of [[Convergence of a sequence]]. 
In other words, a sequence is a Cauchy sequence if, for every $\epsilon$, there is a point in the sequence after which the terms are all closer to each other than the given $\epsilon$. 

Convergent sequences are Cauchy, and Cauchy sequences converge.

Similar to [[Theorem 2.4.2 - Monotone Convergence Theorem]], it's another way of proving that sequences converge without knowing what the limits are.
